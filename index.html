The Physics: Induction

Faraday's Law of Induction

ε = -N ( ΔΦ / Δt )

ε = Induced Voltage
ΔΦ = Change in Flux
Requirement: The field MUST be changing.

The entire project hinges on Faraday's Law. This formula tells us that we can induce an electric potential in the Secondary Coil ONLY IF the Magnetic Flux is changing over time.

This leads to our biggest challenge: a battery creates a static field. We need to force it to change thousands of times per second.

The Circuit Schematic

Battery • Switch • 2N2222A Transistor • 27kΩ Resistor

The circuit is deceptively simple. We have a 9V Battery, a Switch, two Coils, and the heart of the circuit: the 2N2222A Transistor.

The Electronic Switch

2N2222A Transistor

Base (B): The Control (The "Tap").

Collector (C): Power In.

Emitter (E): Ground Out.

Switching ~40,000 times/sec

The transistor acts as a high-speed electronic switch. A small current at the Base opens the gate, allowing a much larger current to flow through the coil. It switches ON and OFF thousands of times per second.

The Feedback Loop

How does it sustain oscillation?

  1. Start: Resistor triggers Base -> Transistor ON.
  2. Field Expands: Current flows through Coil.
  3. Reverse Induction: Coil induces NEGATIVE voltage in Base.
  4. Cutoff: Transistor turns OFF.
  5. Repeat: Field collapses -> Cycle restarts.
How does the transistor know when to turn OFF? It tells itself! 1. Transistor turns ON. 2. Magnetic field expands. 3. This expansion induces a REVERSE voltage that turns the transistor OFF. 4. The field collapses, and the resistor turns it back ON.

Engineering Challenge

The "Resistor Incident"

Problem: Transistor Overheating & No Light.

Diagnosis: Base current was too high (Saturation).

Solution: The 27kΩ Resistor.

This specific resistance provides the perfect "Bias" to allow oscillation.

Initially, the circuit didn't work. The transistor would get incredibly hot. I realized the current flowing into the Base was too strong. Through troubleshooting, I confirmed that using the correct 27kÎ© was the only way to get a stable, working circuit.

Construction & Fatigue

  • No PCB: "Twist-and-Tape" connections.
  • The Center Tap: Required sanding off enamel insulation.
  • Component Fatigue: Broken transistor pins required replacement.
Building this without soldering presented challenges. A single loose wire at the Base or Center Tap would kill the oscillation. I also had to carefully sand off the enamel on the wire to make electrical contact.
